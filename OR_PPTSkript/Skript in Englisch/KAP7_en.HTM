7. Transport models, transshipment problems, allocation models


7.1 The transport model

We speak of a transport problem if the following setting of a task is given:

Goods in the quantities a1, a2, ..., an are produced at n different production sites (A1,A2, ... An). Further, there are m demand places (B1, B2, ..., Bm) where the produced goods are needed in the quantities b1, b2, ..., bm.

From the point of view of minimising the transport costs, it obviously matters which quantities from which production sites are transported to which demand places. Altogether, there are n x m transport routes, which is illustrated in fig. 16 (n = 3, m = 5):


Fig. 16: transport routes for 3 production sites and 5 demand places

Let each individual transport route be allocated to a cost factor that does not depend on quantity; this cost factor is indicated by cik (i = 1,...,n; k = 1,...,m).
We further presuppose that the whole amount of production is equal to the whole amount in demand. With the aid of Linear Programming, we can formulate the task as follows, if we indicate the unknown transport quantities with xik:

objective function:
  cik Xik Min

restrictions:
  Xik = a1     (i = 1,...,n)
  Xik = bk     (k = 1,...,m)
  bk = ai


non negativity restriction: xik 0 (for all i and all k)

In contrast to the tasks of Linear Programming, we have so far seen that we can state two things:

First, the restrictions are given in the form of equations. Second, only the coefficients 0 and 1 occur in the restrictions.
These unusual features suggest that we do not employ the Simplex method for solving the transport problem, but opt for a specific - easier - alternative. This alternative shall be illustrated with a numerical example. The following specific problem shall be given:

Three production sites A1, A2, A3 with the quantities a1 = 60, a2 = 35, a3 = 40. Further, five demand places B1,...,B5 with b1 = 22, b2 = 45, b3 = 20, b4 = 18, b5 = 30.
The cost factors cik are each given in the scheme (fig. 17). What we want are the transport quantities Xik (i = 1,...,3; k = 1,...,5), that lead to minimal transport costs.



Fig. 17: schematic depiction of the transport connections with corresponding cost factors.


Similar to the Simplex method, where we strive for the optimal solution through iteration, starting from an initial basic solution, here, we also look for a basic solution that we can then improve step by step. Three questions need to be taken into account:

1. How do we get an initial solution ?

2. How do we know what an optimal solution is ?

3. How can we achieve an improved solution ?

7.1.1 Initial solution

Generally, nothing prevents us from starting with an arbitrary initial solution. Nevertheless, it is not entirely arbitrary on which quantity distribution this is based. An "arbitrary initial solution" can be found by employing the north-west corner method. This method begins the allocation at the top left corner (hence north-west), covering (if possible) the demands of B1. Then the remainder of the production of A1 (if such a remainder still exists) is distributed onto B2, etc., moving along the diagonal. Eventually, we get the quantity distribution illustrated in fig. 18.


Fig. 18: initial solution employing the north-west method


This method is easy to handle, but in comparison to another method, which we shall discuss later, we see that the initial position is rather weak, because this procedure has not taken into account the objective of minimising the transport costs.

In the following, we are therefore going to illustrate a method that within a row of the scheme allocates, in order, reduced cost factors to the demand places. Then it goes on to the next row: method with fixed row order. The result of this procedure is depicted in fig. 19.



Fig. 19: initial solution employing the method with fixed row order,
with the "remaining quantities" i = ai - xik   and   k = bk - xik

The individual numbers yield as follows, beginning with the first row of the cost matrix:

The position A1/B2 has the lowest cost factor. The demand of B2 is b2 = 45. This demand can be met by the production of A1, namely a1 = 60. With x12 = 45, we get 2 = 0 and 1 = 15. The next highest cost factor can be found in A1/B3. The demand of B3, namely b3 = 20, however, cannot be fully met by the remaining 1 = 15; therefore we get x13 = 15. This leads to 3 = 5 and 1 = 0. That means we have completed row 1, because the production of A1 has completely been reduced.

In the second row, we begin with A2/B1, let x21 = 22, 1 = 0, and 2 = 13. Then x23 = 5, 3 = 0, and 2 = 8. Eventually, x24 = 8, 4 = 10, and 2 = 0. That means that the production of A2 has also become zero.

The third row is treated in the same way.


This second initial solution is usually better than the one we get with the north-west corner method, because the latter amounts to a total cost of K1 = cij xij = 363, whereas here we get K2 = 320.

Now we can see why the first solution was described as weak, in the sense of being "too far away from the optimal solution". As we will transform the initial solution, improving it step by step, into the optimal solution, and understandably keeping the number of steps to a minimum, it is obvious that we select the best initial solution as basis/base, here the solution with the method of fixed row order.

7.1.2 Stepping stone method

If we now want to improve the current solution, we can only do so by altering the quantity distribution plan that we have arranged. This alteration, of course, needs to be carried out with the objective of reducing the total transport costs. The question arises, though, whether the existing transport plan may already be optimal. To ascertain this, we analyse whether we can achieve a reduction of the costs by including transport connections we have so far not considered.
With this aim in view, we select the positions with Xik = 0 in the above schema; we then calculate which costs occur or can be saved, if we let these Xik = 1. This arrangement, though, affects the restrictions, because the corresponding ai and bk are also both increased by 1.
To adjust this, we need to subtract the amount 1 from the already existing transport amounts. An example (position A1/B1) from the above schema will illustrate this:
Adding 1 in A1/B1 implies subtracting 1 in A1/B3. Therefore, we need to increase A2/B3 by 1, which, in turn, implies subtracting 1 in A2/B1 (see fig. 20). For this "jumping" over occupied positions, there is always only one position per route.


Fig. 20: alteration of the quantity distribution with activation
of a further transport route.

Adding and subtracting 1, respectively, is balanced, i.e. the restrictions are not affected. Of interest now are the new costs, which are generally indicated by Vik; thus, in our case, V11:

    V11 = 1 * c11 - 1 * c13 + 1 * c23 - 1 * c21
    V11 = 4 - 3 + 2 - 2
    V11 = 1

In this case, the costs have been increased, that is by including these transport connections that have so far not been considered, additional costs of one transportation unit per quantity unit occur. The objective, however, was to save costs.
The above method now needs to be carried out for all positions where xik = 0. This yields to the scheme (fig. 21) where the vik are printed in small.



Fig. 21: initial solution with inserted coefficients Vk .
In the framed position lies min (Vk)

We can see that saving is only possible in two positions, namely A3/B1 (V31 = -1) and A3/B3 (V33 = -2). If we limit each iteration step to one alteration, we will, of course, attempt to transport as many quantity units as possible along the route A3/B3, as we can save the most here. However, we have an upper limit, since, due to the restrictions, we also need to alter the quantities in those positions whose cost factors have been used in ascertaining V33. Even here, we still cannot proceed arbitrarily. If, for example, we want to transfer six units from A3 to B3, we would need to have - for not exceeding the demand of B3, namely 20 - the value -1 in position A2/B3; this, however, violates the non-negativity retriction. Generally we can say that the new xik is only permitted to be as high as the lowest amount whose cost factor with negative sign was included in calculating V33; thus, on our case, x33 = 5.

Inserting this, while simultaneously altering the other positions in such a way that the restrictions are still satisfied, yields the first improved solution (the new Vik have already been entered) in the schema (fig. 22).



Fig. 22: first improved solution


For this improved solution, K = 310 yields, that is a reduction by 10 transportation units. However, we can see from the negative Vik, that an improvement is possible again. (The additional arrows in the last schema illustrate the route that has been followed for the slightly more difficult calculation of V22).

The method outlined here:

  • calculation of Vik, and
  • activation of transport connections with min (Vik)

is the so-called stepping stone method.

We now want to further improve the scheme (fig. 22). We only indicate the results in tabular form, without a detailed outline of the individual operations (fig. 23).

To summarise the procedure:

1. How do we get an initial solution ?
According to the "method with fixed row order", we allocate - beginning with the first row (i = 1) - the highest possible amount to the transport connection with min (cik); then the position with the next highest cik to the remainder of the production, etc.

2. How do we know what an optimal solution is ?
For all the positions where xik = 0, we calculate the value Vik. As long as there is at least one Vik < 0, the solution is not optimal (part 1 of the stepping stone method).

3. How can we achieve an improved solution ?
We allocate a preferably high amount to the transport connection with min (Vik). the new xik is only permitted to be as high as the lowest amount whose cost factor cik has been included in calculating the min (Vik) with negative sign (part2 of the stepping stone method).


7.1.3 Additional thoughts

In the setting of the task, we presupposed that the amount of production and the amount of demand were equal. This precondition is not always given, and we do not want to analyse the case where the the amount of production is higher than the total demand. In numbers: a1 = 60, a2 = 100, a3 = 40; b1 = 22, b2 = 45, b3 = 20, b4 = 18, b5 = 30. The production exceeds the demand by 65 transportation units. Applying the convention of Linear Programming yields:

objective function:

  cik xik Min

restrictions:

  xik ai     (i = 1,2,3)
  xik = bk    (k = 1,...,5)


non negativity conditions:

xik 0 (for all i and all k)

To be able to apply the stepping stone method here as well, we need to consider the following:

If the production is higher than the demand, it only makes sense that quantities are stocked at the production sites. In order to ascertain these quantities, we can establish a fictive storage place L that serves as a common storage for the 3 production sites A1, A2, A3. The quantities that are "transported" to L from A1, A2, A3 will, of course, be included in the calculation with the cost factors zero. In reality, they are the quantities that are kept at the production sites.

The two schemes (fig. 26 and 27) depict the initial solution and the optimal solution, which is achieved after 4 iteration steps.



Fig. 26: initial solution


To get a better idea, we have left out the Vik in this depiction. The result can be interpreted as follows:
15 transprotation units remain at production site A1, 30 at A2, and 20 at A3. The rest in each production site is distributed to the demand places according to the above scheme.

This example presupposed that the amount of production is higher than the amount of demand. Of course, this can also be the other way round. In that case, we establish a fictive production site to which we allocate the deficit of the total amount. By including this fictive production site into the calculation schema, the final result yields quantities that need to be transported from these fictive production sites to the individual demand places. We then interpret these quantities as the deficit of the individual demand places.

7.2 The transshipment problem

7.3 The allocation model

Allocation models are defined in analogy to the transport models. However, the so-called individual rows are provided with " = 1 ".

The method bank contains a program (named Zuordnungsplanung) that outlines the allocation planning in the personnel sector. The program generates an LP formulation and calls an external LP program (solver) for the calculation.
