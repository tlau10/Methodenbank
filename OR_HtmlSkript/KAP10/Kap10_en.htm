10. The offcut problem

The offcut problem, in terms of the setting of the task, is a wholly different cattle of fish; there are two variants, a one-dimensional and a two-dimensional offcut problem. Both settings of the task have in common that previous to formulating the linear model, and therefore also previous to the solution with the Simplex method, we need to determine the so-called cutting variants.


10.1 The one-dimensional offcut-problem

Here, the idea is to manufacture strips with a given width and a given number of reels with a certain width in such a way that the offcut is minimal (see fig. 28).


Fig. 28: reel distribution with the one-dimensional cutting problem



An example shall explain this:

As source material w have reels with a width of 3,000 mm. The following order is given, with the following required cuttings:

250 reels with a width of 900 mm
150 reels with a width of 690 mm
120 reels with a width of 280 mm


Question: how does the source material need to be "cut" to fulfil the order - with minimal offcut?

To get a first overview of the possible cutting variants, the table in figure 29 is a good aid: it is constructed as follows:

First, we try (1. row) to "fit in" the largest width that has been ordered as often as possible. This leads to an offcut of 300 mm. Then (2. row) we reduce the number of pieces for the largest width by one and try to fit it in the next width as often as possible. We see that this way, the number of pieces steadily follows along the diagonal from left to right. There are 12 combinations altogether, and their frequency shall be decided by x1, ..., x12.

Now to establish the linear model:

objective function: the objective is to minimise the offcut. The total offcut is the sum of the product of reel length (L), width of the offcut and (unknown) number of pieces (xi).

Z* = L 300 x1 + L 125 x2 + ... + L 305 x12 Min

Dividing by L yields:

Z = 300 x1 + 125 x2 + ... + 305 x12 Min


The restrictions, due to combinatory reasons, can usually not be established in form of equations: 

3x1 + 2x2 + 2x3 + x4 + x5 + x6 + x7                   ≥ 250
x2 + 3x4 + 2x5 + x6 + 4x8 + 3x9 + 2x10 + x11          ≥ 150
x2 + 3x3 + x5 + 3x6 + 5x7 + 2x9 + 4x10 + 6x11 + 7x12  ≥ 120

The non-negativity restrictions: 

xi ≥ 0
  (i = 1,...,12)

As our prime interest lies in the setting of the task and the mathematical formulation, we can neglect the solution here.

The method bank program Verschnittplanung (offcut planning) encapsulates the aforementioned mathematical formulation of the problem as user programs.


The same problem has also been solved employing a genetic algorithm program: GA-Verschnittplanung
(see also OR-Methodenbank InfoSystem, Verzeichnis AND).


10.2 The two-dimensional offcut problem

Here the idea is to gain products of a given length and width with minimal offcut from metal slabs. To have a concrete setting of the task, the following numbers are given:

source product are slabs with the measurements 2m x 1m.

To deliver is the following:

100 slabs 0,5 x 0,6 m (type of slap I),
  50 slabs 0,8 x 0,4 m (type of slap II),
100 slabs 0,3 x 1,0 m (type of slap III).

What we want is the offcut plan with minimal offcut!


Before attending to the mathematical formulation, we first need to decide on the "cutting variants" here, too. This problem has nothing to do with Linear programming, and we only want to consider - for not stretching the setting of the task too far - the 6 cutting variants listed (fig. 30). The majority of the other variants are excluded for reasons concerning production.

First the objective function:

Z* = 0,20x1 + 0,16x2 + 0,16x3 + 0,40.x4 + 0,16x5 + 0,20x6 Min

The requirements for keeping the offcut minimal can be formulated - slightly simplified - as follows: the number of initially required metal slabs is to be kept as low as possible; this leads to the following objective function:

Z = x1 + x2 + x3 + x4 + x5 + x6 Min

The restrictions yield from the following reflection: we want to manufacture at least 100 pieces of the type of slab I, but for this we can only use the variants V1, V3, V5, that is:

6x1 + 2x3 + 4x5 ≥ 100


Analogously for the types of slab II and III:


2 x2 + 2 x3 + 5 x4 + 2 x5 ≥ 50

4 x2 + 2 x3 + 6 x6 ≥ 100


Non-negativity condition:

xi ≥ 0 (i = 1,...,6)


The solution employing the Simplex method is:

x1 = 17, x2 = 25 ;

That is, 17 slabs of variant I and 25 slabs of variant II result in minimal offcut (7.4 m2).
